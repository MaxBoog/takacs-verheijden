Academia knows no boundaries. Your research is never finished, and your teaching can always be Academic leadership: what does that entail? A career in science is very demanding. improved. At the same time the necessity to do research in teams and consortia has greatly increased. Developing Leadership

Scientists are intrinsically very motivated to give their best in education, research patient care. But sometimes the best intentions have unintended and even disastrous effects. Self-reflection, feedback, peer consultation, and open communication are helpful to monitor and assess yourself and thus create space for a safe environment for both employees and students. Those in leadership positions play a crucial role here.

Curious how others do it, how other faculties are organized? How to manage without losing sight of your own interests? For this type of questions and dilemma’s we tailor leadership programs.

Our Academic Leadership program is a solid and personal journey into the three main aspects of leadership:

  1. Strategic: Doing the right things. What are your ambitions, what goals do you want to achieve and how will you stay focused?
  2. Operational: Doing things right. How do you implement your goals in everyday reality and how do you keep your employees engaged and enthusiastic?
  3. Systemic: Influence the context. How do you read the world outside? How do you involve your stakeholders and make sure your have allies to support your plans?

The real questions and issues of our participants are always central, complemented by directly applicable models and theory. The course is offered either integral or modular, depending on demand.

Intervision

Intervision is a central tool in all our activities. Two assumptions play an important role: every problem you face also has a link with yourself, because someone else in your position might not have it at all. And secondly: every problem is embedded in a context.

Here's how it works: you explain your question or dilemma to your colleague-participants. The external facilitator uses a rather strict method to reveal the potential question behind the question. Then, at some point, the other participants express what they think might be the real problem and what they would do in your place. Often the other participants recognize your question – and benefit themselves also from the whole process.

Intervision promotes self-reflection. Often participants find it liberating to realize that they are not alone in dealing with questions and uncertainties. In Academia an open and vulnerable expression of doubts was often interpreted as weakness. In intervision, the common wisdom of a group of people is catalyzed and exploited to the full.

Coaching

It can be very effective to discuss your questions and dilemmas with someone who knows your world, but who does not belong to it. A listening ear, a sounding board. Someone to you can be completely open to, who will listen without judgment. But who will also call a spade a spade and may give you honest feedback or concrete advice for your next steps.

Coaching often highlights very personal experiences and beliefs that affect the professional development of a person. A listening ear, but also someone who asks and does not judge, can help to detect and face the real issue. The meetings, face-to-face or online, are completely confidential. A coaching program usually consists of four or five sessions of one to one and a half hours.

Advies

Our expertise is facilitating leadership as a process. Scientists themselves have the content expertise and know very well where they want to go. But how to get there? With a team of highly intelligent and sometimes quite stubborn colleagues for whom autonomy is crucial?

We can support you in several ways. As an outside sparring partner, for instance. Together we design a plan and discuss the next step as we go. The advantage is that you are visible to the others as the one in charge. But we can also facilitate one or several meetings, to help the team to take new steps together. The advantage of this method is that you can then participate in content yourself.

Recent advice trajectories entailed:

  • Building a psychologically safe working environment after years of incidents.
  • Developing a shared vision of research that addresses not just the content but also the code of conduct for all involved.
  • Completing a process of mourning and creating a new start as a team.
  • Developing a shared strategy with a group of top researchers all over the world.
  • Designing and implementing an ‘inclusive culture trajectory’ for a large department together with a sound board group
  • Facilitating education innovation to reduce workload of teachers and motivate students.
